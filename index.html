Nutritional Racism in Memphis

They call it "unconcious bias"

Intro

Health and Nutrition are a major part of human development and should always come first. Eating healthy can lead to people becoming productive citizens who engage in the word around them. However, Memphis has put the health of its citizens last.

The heavy amounts of unconscious bias within the city has cause African- Americans to be placed under higher amounts of stress and less likely to receive outside help from a physician. Basically, being black is bad for your health.

Hardcore Facts

An alarming 38.2% of Americans are obese or overweight.

Tennessee ranked 6th for diabetes and 7th for hypertension.

Advertistiment and Marketing

Fast food and soda companies purposely advertise to blacks. Using black models and hip-hop music in commercials are all ways for companies to entice blacks to eating unhealthily. On the other side companies that produce health conscious foods advertise to whites. All four of these companies use stereotypes of races within Memphis to promote their brand.

The Children are the Future. . .

Education

Power Score Test Preparation list “eat dinner” as the second most important task to complete the night before taking a major exam like the ACT(ACT). The site explains that test takers should eat a meal high in carbohydrates. If lower-income families do not have access to the nutrition they need then how are they going to compete against children who are able to eat a balanced breakfast and dinner. Nutritional racism already sets children back and farther away from their goals.

GEMS

In 2010, The Memphis Girls' Health Enrichment Multi-site Studies (GEMS) developed a 2-year evaluation to view obesity prevention programs and their efforts on African- American girls. By the end of the 2-years the experts suggested that the girls needed to reduce their sugar intakes by drinking less sodas. However, these dietary changes seemed to be more successful in the younger girls which means obesity prevention should start before the teen years.
